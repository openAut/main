# Example: Office with High CO₂ Levels

## Situation
Employees in an open‑plan office (500 m², occupancy 40 people) complain of drowsiness and headaches in the afternoon. The building uses a VAV system with demand control based on CO₂ sensors. The building is ten years old and has not undergone recent renovations.

## Likely causes
1. **Faulty or uncalibrated CO₂ sensors** – if sensors read low values, the control system may reduce airflow, leading to high actual CO₂ levels.
2. **Insufficient minimum airflow** – VAV boxes or dampers may be closing too far, resulting in airflows below the recommended 7 l/s per person plus 0.35 l/s·m²(AFS 2020:1 §22).
3. **Blocked or dirty filters** – reduced airflow due to clogged filters can elevate CO₂.
4. **High occupant density** – occupancy may exceed design assumptions, requiring higher ventilation.

## Controls and measurements
- **Measure CO₂**: Use portable CO₂ loggers at multiple locations to verify concentrations; compare to the 1 000 ppm guideline(FoHMFS 2014:18, avsnitt CO₂).
- **Verify sensor calibration**: Check calibration dates; compare BMS sensor readings to portable instruments.
- **Measure supply airflows**: Use anemometers or flow hoods at diffusers; ensure each zone meets minimum per‑person and per‑area flow rates(AFS 2020:1 §22).
- **Check damper positions**: Inspect VAV dampers and actuators; ensure they do not close below a minimum setting.
- **Review schedules**: Confirm that ventilation increases before occupancy and maintains sufficient flow during peak periods.

## Recommended actions
1. Calibrate or replace CO₂ sensors; schedule regular calibration.
2. Adjust VAV minimum airflow setpoints to meet or exceed 7 l/s per person + 0.35 l/s·m²(AFS 2020:1 §22).
3. Replace dirty filters and inspect supply and return fans.
4. Review occupancy levels; if consistently higher than design, re‑calculate ventilation requirements and consider system upgrades.
5. Provide occupants with information on the importance of not blocking supply diffusers or return grilles.
6. Monitor CO₂ and ventilation trends to verify improvement.

## Energy impact
Potentially high: Incorrect demand‑controlled ventilation may have reduced airflow excessively to save energy, resulting in poor IAQ. Increasing minimum flows will consume more energy, but proper demand control and sensor calibration can minimise unnecessary ventilation.

## Regulation and compliance notes
- CO₂ concentrations should generally remain below 1 000 ppm(FoHMFS 2014:18, avsnitt CO₂).
- Ventilation must provide at least 7 l/s per person and 0.35 l/s·m² in offices(AFS 2020:1 §22).
- Follow Arbetsmiljöverket guidelines for indoor air quality and ventilation.

## Risks and assumptions
- If sensors are faulty, reliance on demand control may have systematically under‑ventilated the space.
- Adjustments may reveal limitations of the existing system capacity.
- Additional cooling may be needed if increased ventilation raises heat load.

## Questions for precision
- What are the design ventilation rates and system capacity?
- When were CO₂ sensors last calibrated?
- Are there local heat sources or pollutant sources that may require higher ventilation?
