# Example: Hydronic Heating Imbalance

## Situation
A five‑storey office building with a hydronic radiator system heated by district heating experiences uneven temperatures: top floors are cold while lower floors are overheated. The building’s heating system uses a variable-speed pump but has not been rebalanced since renovations.

## Likely causes
1. **Unbalanced flow distribution** – balancing valves may be improperly set, causing too much flow on lower floors and too little on upper floors【988802230460612†L154-L159】.
2. **Insufficient pump head or incorrect pump control** – pump may not provide adequate pressure to overcome vertical height; variable-speed control may be set incorrectly.
3. **Air in the system** – trapped air at high points can block water flow to upper floors.
4. **Stuck or malfunctioning control valves** – thermostatic radiator valves may be stuck closed or open.

## Controls and measurements
- **Measure supply and return temperatures** on each floor; low ΔT on lower floors and high ΔT on upper floors indicate imbalance.
- **Check differential pressure** across balancing valves and risers.
- **Bleed air** from radiators and high points to remove trapped air.
- **Inspect pump curve**: Compare pump differential pressure to required head; adjust speed if necessary.
- **Verify balancing valves**: Use flow meters to measure flows at riser and branch levels; adjust valves according to design.
- **Examine radiator valves**: Ensure thermostatic valves operate smoothly.

## Recommended actions
1. Bleed air from the system.
2. Perform hydronic balancing: adjust balancing valves at each riser and radiator to match design flows【988802230460612†L154-L159】.
3. Check and adjust pump speed or replace pump if unable to provide required head.
4. Repair or replace faulty thermostatic valves.
5. Insulate piping where necessary to reduce heat loss.
6. Document settings and provide maintenance staff with balancing records.

## Energy impact
High: Unbalanced hydronic systems waste energy by overheating some areas while under‑heating others, leading occupants to open windows or use supplemental heaters. Proper balancing will reduce return temperatures, improving district heating efficiency.

## Regulation and compliance notes
- Ensure indoor temperatures meet comfort requirements (typically 20–24 °C).
- Check that hydronic system operation does not conflict with energy efficiency guidelines.
- Hydronic balancing is part of good commissioning and maintenance practices.

## Risks and assumptions
- Balancing requires skilled technicians and measurement equipment.
- Adjustments may temporarily affect comfort.
- Additional insulation may be needed.

## Questions for precision
- What are the design flow rates and temperatures for each riser?
- Has the pump been replaced or modified recently?
- Are thermostatic valves original or have they been upgraded?
