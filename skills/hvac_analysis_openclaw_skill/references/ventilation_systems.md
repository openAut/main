# Ventilation Systems

This document outlines common ventilation system types used in Swedish buildings. Understanding system types helps diagnose issues, interpret symptoms, and propose appropriate solutions.

## S – Natural supply (self‑contained)
A **S** system relies on natural draught and infiltration for outdoor air, combined with mechanical exhaust. Fresh air enters through vents in walls or windows, flows through rooms, and leaves via mechanical exhaust fans. Advantages: simple and low energy use. Disadvantages: supply airflow varies with wind and temperature; difficult to control; poor heat recovery. Typical issues include draughts, underventilation, and moisture problems.

## F – Mechanical exhaust (Frånluft)
An **F** system uses mechanical exhaust fans to extract air from wet rooms (bathrooms, kitchens) while outdoor air enters through pressure‑controlled vents in living spaces. There is no mechanical supply. Advantages: low installation cost; minimal ducting. Disadvantages: supply airflow varies with pressure; negative building pressure can draw in radon or pollutants; no heat recovery. Design flows follow Boverket’s minimum 0.35 l/s·m² rule. Energy can be saved with demand‑controlled fans.

## FT – Mechanical supply and exhaust (Tilluft + Frånluft)
An **FT** system provides both mechanical supply and mechanical exhaust. Supply air is delivered to rooms via ducts; exhaust air is mechanically removed. Benefits: better control of airflow and pressure balance. Drawbacks: higher installation cost and energy use. Without heat recovery, supply air may require electric or water heaters to maintain comfort.

## FTX – Mechanical supply and exhaust with heat recovery (Tilluft + Frånluft med värmeväxlare)
An **FTX** system includes a heat exchanger (typically rotary or plate) that recovers heat from exhaust air to preheat supply air, reducing heating energy. FTX systems can also incorporate cooling recovery in summer. They provide good control of airflow and energy efficiency but require maintenance (filters, heat exchanger cleaning). Typical issues: low heat recovery efficiency due to fouled exchanger, imbalanced supply and exhaust, high pressure drop across filters.

## CAV – Constant air volume
**Constant air volume (CAV)** systems deliver a fixed airflow regardless of occupancy or indoor conditions. They are simple and reliable but may over‑ventilate when occupancy is low, wasting energy.

## VAV – Variable air volume
**Variable air volume (VAV)** systems adjust airflow based on occupancy, temperature, or air quality sensors (CO₂, humidity). They use dampers and control logic to modulate supply and exhaust flows, reducing energy use and improving comfort. Challenges include maintaining pressure balance, commissioning damper positions, and ensuring sensor calibration.

## Demand‑controlled ventilation
Demand‑controlled systems use sensors (CO₂, VOC, occupancy) and controls to adjust ventilation flows in real time. They can be implemented in VAV or hybrid systems. Proper sensor placement, calibration, and control algorithms are essential to avoid underventilation or over‑ventilation.
## Radon and pressure balance
In S and F systems, the building is held at negative pressure relative to the outdoors. This can draw radon-laden soil air through cracks in the foundation, particularly in radon-risk areas. Signs of elevated radon risk: old building, crawl-space or direct slab-on-ground construction, high local radon geology. Mitigation typically involves sealing penetrations, adding supply air to improve pressure balance, or installing sub-slab depressurisation. Radon measurements (long-term measurements using track detectors, at least 60 days) are required before and after measures. The Swedish action level is 200 Bq/m³ in existing buildings (Strålsäkerhetsmyndigheten, SSM FS 2018:5).
