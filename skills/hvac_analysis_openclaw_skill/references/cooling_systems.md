# Cooling Systems

Cooling removes heat from indoor spaces to maintain comfort and protect equipment. Common system types include:

## Direct expansion (DX)
DX systems transfer heat directly to a refrigerant via evaporator coils. Air conditioners and split units are examples. Advantages: simple installation, compact. Issues: refrigerant leaks, dirty coils, insufficient airflow, icing, or malfunctioning thermostats. Maintenance must be performed by certified technicians.

## Chilled water systems
In a chilled water system, a chiller produces chilled water, which is pumped to air handling units (AHUs) or fan coil units that cool air. This allows centralised cooling for large buildings. Key components: chiller, pumps, cooling towers (for heat rejection), AHU coils, valves. Problems: fouled heat exchangers, incorrect flow rates, low refrigerant charge, control misalignment. Energy optimisation includes variable-speed drives on pumps and fans, chiller sequencing, and free cooling at low outdoor temperatures.

## Free cooling
When outdoor air is sufficiently cool, free cooling uses outside air directly or via heat exchangers to cool indoor spaces without running chillers. Benefits include reduced energy use. Proper control is necessary to avoid overcooling or moisture problems.

## Split systems
Split systems consist of an indoor unit (evaporator) and an outdoor unit (condenser) connected by refrigerant lines. They provide cooling (and sometimes heating) for individual rooms. Issues include refrigerant leaks, dirty filters, and inadequate drainage. Regular cleaning of filters and coils, and proper sizing, are important.

## Heat pumps in cooling mode
Heat pumps can reverse operation to provide cooling. Efficiency is similar to their heating operation but may decrease at very high outdoor temperatures.

Cooling systems often interact with ventilation (e.g., cooling coils in AHUs). Ensure that supply air temperatures and humidity levels are controlled to prevent condensation and occupant discomfort.
## District cooling (fjärrkyla)
District cooling distributes chilled water from a central plant through insulated pipes to multiple buildings, similar to district heating. It is common in Swedish city centres (e.g., Stockholm, Göteborg). The building receives chilled water via a heat exchanger; the internal hydronic loop is independent of the district network. Advantages: eliminates on-site chillers, high efficiency through economies of scale and use of cold lake/sea water or industrial waste cold. Key considerations: heat exchanger sizing, return temperature control (district operators set limits), and compatibility with building hydronic pressures.

## Absorption cooling
Absorption chillers use heat (e.g., district heating, waste heat, solar thermal) rather than electricity to drive the refrigeration cycle. They are used where cheap heat is available and electricity is expensive or limited. Efficiency is expressed as COP (typically 0.6–1.3 for single-effect systems). Maintenance requirements include water treatment (LiBr-water solution), purging of non-condensable gases, and periodic inspection of the vacuum integrity.
