# Heating Systems

This document summarises common heating systems and typical considerations.

## District heating
District heating supplies hot water or steam from a central plant to buildings via insulated pipes. Advantages: high efficiency due to large‑scale production and potential use of waste heat. Considerations: heat exchanger maintenance, differential pressure control, and compatibility with building hydronic systems. Symptoms of problems include low supply temperatures, high return temperatures (indicating poor heat transfer), and uneven heating.

## Heat pumps
Heat pumps transfer heat from a source (air, ground, water) to the building. Types include air‑to‑water, air‑to‑air, ground source, and exhaust air heat pumps. Key parameters are coefficient of performance (COP), supply and return temperatures, and defrost cycles. Common issues include:
- Frost or ice formation on outdoor coils.
- Low refrigerant charge or leaks (requires certified technician).
- Incorrect sizing or installation.
- Poor airflow across coils.
- Frequent start/stop cycles reducing efficiency.
A properly maintained heat pump can deliver 2–4 times more heat than the electrical energy consumed.

## Boilers
Boilers burn fuels (oil, gas, biomass) to heat water. Efficiency depends on combustion efficiency, flue gas losses, and control. Modern condensing boilers recover latent heat from exhaust gases. Issues include:
- Scaling or corrosion reducing heat transfer.
- Inadequate draft or flue gas venting.
- Faulty burners or safety devices.
Maintenance and periodic inspection are essential.

## Electric heating
Electric resistance heating converts electricity directly to heat. It is simple and responsive but expensive to operate compared with district heating or heat pumps. Electric heaters may be used as supplementary heating or in small buildings without central hydronic systems. Monitor safety and ensure thermostats function properly.

## Hydronic distribution
Hydronic heating uses water circulated by pumps through pipes and radiators or floor loops. Balancing (adjusting flow to each terminal) is critical to ensure proper temperature distribution and energy efficiency. Oversized pumps or improper valve settings can cause noise, high energy use, or uneven heating. See `references/hydronic_systems.md` for more details.
