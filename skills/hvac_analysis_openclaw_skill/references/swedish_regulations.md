# Swedish Regulations and Guidelines

This document summarises key requirements and guidelines from Swedish authorities for ventilation and indoor climate. Sources include Boverket (national building regulations), Folkhälsomyndigheten (public health), Arbetsmiljöverket (work environment), and OVK requirements. Use this information when assessing compliance or recommending improvements.

## Minimum outdoor airflow

- **Residential buildings:** Boverket requires an outdoor air supply of at least **0.35 litres per second per square metre (l/s·m²)** of floor area and not less than **4 l/s per person**(BBR 9:2). If the 0.35 l/s·m² criterion gives a lower value than 4 l/s per person, the higher per‑person requirement applies(FoHMFS 2014:18).

- **Schools and offices:** For sedentary activities, Folkhälsomyndigheten and Arbetsmiljöverket recommend a minimum supply of **7 l/s per person plus 0.35 l/s·m²**(AFS 2020:1 §22). Airflows should be adapted to the activity level; higher flows are needed for more demanding work(AFS 2020:1 §22).

- **Other occupancies:** In public halls and similar spaces, at least 7 l/s per person is recommended(FoHMFS 2014:18). In single‑family houses or small dwellings, infiltration plus mechanical exhaust should meet 0.35 l/s·m².

## CO₂ as an indicator

CO₂ concentration is a useful indicator of ventilation adequacy. Outdoors, CO₂ is around **400 ppm**; well‑ventilated buildings typically have **600–800 ppm**, and levels above **1 000 ppm** suggest insufficient ventilation(FoHMFS 2014:18, avsnitt CO₂). Humans exhale **15–20 litres of CO₂ per hour**, which accumulates indoors if ventilation is inadequate(FoHMFS 2014:18, avsnitt CO₂).

## Humidity guidelines

During the heating season, typical indoor relative humidity is **20–40 %**, with an average around **30 %**(FoHMFS 2014:18, avsnitt fukt). An absolute humidity difference exceeding **3 g/m³** between indoor and outdoor air indicates poor ventilation(FoHMFS 2014:18, avsnitt fukt). Extremely low humidity may cause dry mucous membranes, while high humidity can promote mould and dust mites.

## OVK (Obligatory Ventilation Control)

Swedish law requires periodic function checks of ventilation systems, known as **OVK**. Inspection intervals depend on system type:

- **Public buildings (schools, day-care, healthcare, etc.)** and **multi‑dwelling buildings with supply and extract systems (FT/FTX)**: inspected every **3 years**(PBF 2011:338).
- **Multi‑dwelling or office buildings with exhaust‑only systems (F)**: inspected every **6 years**(PBF 2011:338).
- **Single-family houses with FT/FTX**: only an initial inspection is required(PBF 2011:338).

OVK verifies that systems perform as designed, that airflows meet design values, and that occupants receive instructions. A valid OVK certificate is mandatory for building owners.

## Work environment requirements

Arbetsmiljöverket’s rules emphasise that ventilation flows must remove contaminants produced by humans, equipment, and building materials. For still-sitting work, the **minimum supply is 7 l/s per person plus 0.35 l/s·m²**(AFS 2020:1 §22). CO₂ and humidity should be monitored; relative humidity is generally not regulated but extremely low or high values may affect health(FoHMFS 2014:18, avsnitt fukt). Flows must be adjusted for specific pollutants or heat loads.

## Adaptation to occupancy and activity

Ventilation design must consider occupant density, schedule, and activities. For example, educational spaces, gyms, or dining areas may require higher flows or dedicated exhaust. Airflow balance is critical to avoid negative pressure (which can draw in radon or moisture) or positive pressure (which may drive moisture into envelope). Over‑ventilation increases energy use; therefore, variable air volume (VAV) and demand‑controlled ventilation are encouraged.
